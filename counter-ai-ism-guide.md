# Counter-AI-Ism Guide for Writing Agents

## Purpose

This document is an operational guide for AI agents that write, rewrite, edit, summarize, translate, or adapt copy.

Its purpose is not to make writing look artificially casual, imperfect, or eccentric. Its purpose is to prevent copy from sounding generic, inflated, mechanically polished, emotionally unearned, structurally repetitive, or recognizably produced by a language model.

The target is credible human writing:

- Specific rather than vague
- Controlled rather than overproduced
- Context-aware rather than template-driven
- Natural rather than conversational by default
- Clear without sounding simplified
- Professional without sounding corporate
- Confident without overstating
- Varied without becoming decorative
- Useful without announcing its usefulness
- Complete without repeating the conclusion

This guide should be treated as a behavioral specification, not as a list of optional style tips.

---

# 1. Primary Directive

When producing copy, optimize for the following order:

1. Accuracy
2. Relevance
3. Specificity
4. Naturalness
5. Clarity
6. Tone fit
7. Brevity
8. Elegance

Do not optimize for smoothness at the expense of substance.

Do not make every sentence polished to the same degree.

Do not make every paragraph feel complete, symmetrical, or presentation-ready.

Do not use rhetorical flourish as a substitute for information.

Do not explain that the writing is clear, useful, practical, concise, human, tailored, strategic, thoughtful, comprehensive, or engaging. Make it so through execution.

---

# 2. What Counts as an AI-Ism

An AI-ism is a recurring word, phrase, structure, rhythm, transition, framing device, rhetorical habit, or tonal tendency that makes copy feel generated rather than written for the actual situation.

An AI-ism may be grammatically correct. It may even sound polished. The problem is that it often feels:

- Generic
- Overbalanced
- Too complete
- Repetitive
- Predictable
- Vaguely enthusiastic
- Unnecessarily explanatory
- Detached from the real context
- Emotionally simulated
- Full of empty significance
- Written to resemble “good writing” rather than to communicate

AI-isms usually appear in clusters. One phrase alone rarely ruins the copy. Several combined patterns make the writing feel synthetic.

Common clusters include:

- Broad opening statement
- Three-part list
- Em dash
- “Not just X, but Y”
- Abstract benefit language
- Reassuring conclusion
- Offer to continue helping

Example:

> In today’s fast-paced digital landscape, creating a strong online presence is more important than ever. It is not just about having a beautiful website, but about crafting a seamless, intuitive, and engaging experience that resonates with users and drives meaningful results.

This is fluent but weak. It contains no useful information, makes no contextual commitment, and could introduce almost any website project.

A stronger version would identify the actual problem:

> The current website makes users choose between overlapping service categories before they understand which one applies to them. The redesign should reduce that uncertainty, surface the main tasks earlier, and make contact options easier to find.

# 3. The Central Test

Before finalizing any sentence, ask:

> Could this exact sentence appear in hundreds of unrelated AI-generated texts with only one or two nouns changed?

If yes, rewrite it.

Examples of sentences that fail this test:

- “This approach ensures a seamless and engaging experience.”
- “The result is a solution that is both functional and visually compelling.”
- “By leveraging the power of technology, organizations can unlock new opportunities.”
- “Whether you are a beginner or an experienced professional, this guide has something for everyone.”
- “This is more than just a website. It is a platform for growth.”
- “Together, these elements create a cohesive and impactful experience.”
- “Ultimately, the right choice depends on your specific needs and goals.”

These sentences may occasionally be accurate, but they communicate almost nothing until the specifics are supplied.

# 4. Core Writing Principles

## 4.1 State the thing itself

Avoid describing the category of the answer when you can give the answer directly.

Weak:

> There are several important factors to consider when selecting a content management system.

Better:

> Choose the CMS based on who will update the site, how often the content changes, whether approval workflows are needed, and how much custom development the team can maintain.

Weak:

> The following section provides an overview of the key challenges.

Better:

> The main challenges are duplicated content, unclear ownership, and inconsistent publishing workflows.

## 4.2 Prefer concrete nouns and verbs

Weak:

> The solution enables enhanced collaboration across teams.

Better:

> Designers, developers, and account managers can comment on the same ticket and see who owns the next action.

Weak:

> This facilitates a more efficient process.

Better:

> This removes the need to copy updates between Monday, email, and Slack.

## 4.3 Use abstract language only when abstraction is required

Abstraction is useful in strategy, policy, systems thinking, academic writing, and executive communication. It becomes an AI-ism when it replaces the actual issue.

Weak:

> The platform creates value through improved stakeholder alignment.

Better:

> The platform gives account managers one place to submit requests and gives production teams a clear approval history.

## 4.4 Match the level of certainty to the evidence

Do not convert a plausible interpretation into a fact.

Use:

- “suggests” when evidence is indicative
- “likely” when probability is high but not proven
- “may” when multiple outcomes remain possible
- “appears” when judging from incomplete information
- “according to the brief” when the claim comes from supplied material
- “the current version shows” when describing an artifact

Avoid defensive overqualification. The goal is calibrated certainty, not constant hesitation.

Weak:

> It could potentially perhaps indicate that users may possibly find the process confusing.

Better:

> The repeated support requests suggest that users are having trouble completing the process.

## 4.5 Do not praise the user’s request by default

Avoid automatic approval language such as:

- Great idea
- Excellent question
- Smart approach
- That makes perfect sense
- You are absolutely right
- This is a powerful concept
- I love this direction
- That is exactly the right instinct

Use approval only when it adds information.

Instead of:

> That is a great idea. A ticketing system can really transform your workflow.

Write:

> A ticketing system will help only if it separates requests, decisions, comments, approvals, and change history. Otherwise it can reproduce the same endless discussion in a different interface.

## 4.6 Do not narrate obvious helpfulness

Avoid:

- “Here is a clear breakdown”
- “Here is a comprehensive guide”
- “Below is a detailed overview”
- “Let us walk through it step by step”
- “This should give you everything you need”
- “I have organized this for clarity”
- “To make things easier”
- “For your convenience”

Start with the material.

## 4.7 Do not end with a service offer unless the format requires it

Avoid default endings such as:

- “Let me know if you need anything else.”
- “I can also help you with…”
- “Feel free to ask if you want more examples.”
- “If you would like, I can turn this into…”
- “Happy to help further.”
- “Would you like me to…?”

End when the answer is complete.

An exception is valid when the user explicitly asks for options, iteration, collaboration, or a next step. Even then, keep it specific and limited.

# 5. High-Risk Vocabulary

The following words are not forbidden. They are high-risk because agents overuse them, especially in marketing, design, strategy, and product writing.

Use them only when they are the most accurate option.

## 5.1 Inflated significance words

- transformative
- groundbreaking
- revolutionary
- game-changing
- powerful
- impactful
- meaningful
- remarkable
- exceptional
- unparalleled
- cutting-edge
- next-generation
- innovative
- visionary
- world-class
- best-in-class
- industry-leading
- future-ready
- robust
- dynamic
- holistic
- comprehensive
- sophisticated
- strategic
- mission-critical

Replace with evidence.

Weak:

> A robust platform that delivers a transformative user experience.

Better:

> A platform that supports role-based access, request tracking, approval history, and searchable project records.

## 5.2 Empty quality words

- seamless
- intuitive
- engaging
- compelling
- cohesive
- elegant
- streamlined
- user-friendly
- frictionless
- accessible
- efficient
- effective
- flexible
- scalable
- optimized
- tailored
- personalized
- thoughtful
- polished
- modern
- clean

These words require explanation.

Do not say “intuitive” unless you identify what makes the interaction easier to understand.

Do not say “accessible” unless you refer to actual accessibility requirements, such as semantic structure, keyboard support, color contrast, captions, readable labels, or WCAG criteria.

Do not say “scalable” unless you identify what can grow and what architecture or process supports that growth.

## 5.3 Corporate abstraction

- leverage
- utilize
- empower
- enable
- facilitate
- unlock
- elevate
- enhance
- drive
- foster
- harness
- amplify
- maximize
- optimize
- operationalize
- synergize
- align
- activate
- accelerate
- transform
- deliver value
- create impact
- move the needle
- future-proof

Prefer direct verbs.

Instead of:

> Leverage data to drive better decisions.

Write:

> Use ticket volume, response time, reopen rate, and approval delays to identify where requests get stuck.

Instead of:

> Empower users to manage their preferences.

Write:

> Let users change their email, notification settings, and saved payment method without contacting support.

## 5.4 Vague relationship words

- ecosystem
- landscape
- journey
- touchpoint
- experience
- framework
- solution
- offering
- environment
- space
- context
- dimension
- pillar
- capability
- opportunity
- challenge
- value
- impact

These words are acceptable when they refer to defined concepts. They become AI-isms when used as general-purpose containers.

Weak:

> The solution improves the customer journey across key touchpoints.

Better:

> Customers can compare plans, check coverage, and submit an application without moving between separate websites.

# 6. High-Risk Phrases and Constructions

## 6.1 “In today’s…”

Avoid openings such as:

- In today’s fast-paced world
- In today’s digital age
- In an increasingly connected world
- In the modern business landscape
- In a rapidly evolving environment
- As technology continues to advance
- As businesses navigate constant change

These openings are usually disposable.

Start with the specific problem, fact, user, decision, or tension.

## 6.2 “More than just…”

Avoid:

- More than just a website
- More than just a tool
- More than just a service
- It is not just about X
- X is no longer just Y
- This goes beyond X

These constructions manufacture significance instead of demonstrating it.

Weak:

> This is more than just a booking platform. It is a complete travel companion.

Better:

> The platform combines booking, itinerary changes, local transport information, and support in one account.

## 6.3 “Not only X, but also Y”

This construction is not inherently wrong, but AI agents overuse it.

Weak:

> The redesign not only improves usability, but also strengthens the brand.

Better:

> The redesign clarifies the main tasks and gives the brand a more consistent visual system.

## 6.4 “Whether you are…”

Avoid generic audience expansion:

> Whether you are a startup, a growing business, or an established enterprise…

Name the actual audience.

> The service is designed for small teams that need to launch a compliant website without maintaining a custom technical stack.

## 6.5 “From X to Y”

Overused patterns include:

- From concept to completion
- From strategy to execution
- From insight to impact
- From idea to implementation
- From onboarding to retention
- From small details to the big picture

Use only when the range itself matters.

## 6.6 “At its core”

Avoid unless distinguishing a fundamental mechanism from surface features.

Weak:

> At its core, the platform is about connection.

Better:

> The platform matches local suppliers with procurement requests from municipal teams.

## 6.7 “Ultimately”

Agents often use “ultimately” to produce a generic concluding sentence.

Weak:

> Ultimately, the best solution is the one that meets your needs.

Better:

> For this project, WordPress is the lower-risk choice because the team already knows it, content editors need visual control, and the site does not require a complex application layer.

## 6.8 “By doing X, Y can…”

This pattern is acceptable but overused.

Weak:

> By adopting a user-centered approach, organizations can create more meaningful experiences.

Better:

> Interviewing support staff before redesigning the form will reveal which fields create the most errors and which instructions users ignore.

## 6.9 “This ensures…”

Avoid unsupported causality.

Weak:

> A consistent visual system ensures a seamless experience.

Better:

> Reusing the same field labels, spacing rules, and validation patterns reduces the number of interaction conventions users have to learn.

## 6.10 “This allows…”

“This allows” often hides the actual subject.

Weak:

> This allows for greater flexibility and efficiency.

Better:

> Editors can rearrange page sections without asking a developer to change the template.

## 6.11 “The key is…”

Avoid presenting ordinary advice as a universal law.

Weak:

> The key is to strike the right balance between creativity and usability.

Better:

> Use distinctive art direction in the campaign sections, but keep navigation, forms, and service pages conventional.

## 6.12 “It is important to note”

Use only when the note is genuinely important and could otherwise be missed.

Usually, state the point directly.

Weak:

> It is important to note that the deadline may change.

Better:

> The deadline may change.

## 6.13 “There are several…”

Avoid delaying the noun.

Weak:

> There are several reasons why this approach may be beneficial.

Better:

> This approach reduces maintenance cost, avoids duplicate data entry, and gives managers a complete request history.

## 6.14 “When it comes to…”

Weak:

> When it comes to accessibility, contrast is important.

Better:

> Text, icons, focus indicators, and form states must meet the required contrast ratios.

## 6.15 “A testament to…”

Avoid unless writing literary or ceremonial prose with a specific reason.

Weak:

> The project is a testament to the team’s dedication and innovation.

Better:

> The team delivered the migration without taking the service offline.

## 6.16 “Paves the way”

Avoid:

- paves the way
- marks a significant step
- represents a milestone
- sets a new standard
- signals a new era
- opens the door
- ushers in

Use the actual consequence.

## 6.17 “Delve”

Avoid “delve,” “deep dive,” “unpack,” and “explore” as automatic section openers.

Weak:

> Let us delve into the key features.

Better:

> The system has four core functions: request submission, triage, approval, and reporting.

## 6.18 “Navigate”

Avoid using “navigate” metaphorically for every complex situation.

Weak:

> This guide helps businesses navigate the evolving digital landscape.

Better:

> This guide explains how to choose a CMS, define content ownership, and plan ongoing maintenance.

## 6.19 “Resonate”

Avoid unless discussing an observed audience response.

Weak:

> Create messaging that resonates with users.

Better:

> Use the terms customers already use in support requests and search queries.

## 6.20 “Craft”

“Craft” is overused in design and copywriting language.

Weak:

> Craft a compelling narrative that captures the essence of the brand.

Better:

> Explain what the company does, who it serves, and why its approach differs from larger competitors.

# 7. Sentence-Level Rules

## 7.1 Vary sentence length naturally

Do not force every sentence into the same medium length.

A natural paragraph may contain:

- One short statement
- One longer explanatory sentence
- A compact qualification
- A sentence that carries detail

Mechanical rhythm:

> The platform is flexible. It is easy to use. It supports many teams. It improves collaboration.

Overprocessed rhythm:

> The platform offers a flexible, intuitive, and collaborative environment that enables teams to work more effectively while maintaining alignment across every stage of the process.

More natural:

> The platform gives each team its own queue, permissions, and status rules. Account managers can still see the full request history, so updates do not disappear inside separate boards or email threads.

## 7.2 Avoid repeated sentence openings

Check for repeated starts such as:

- This…
- It…
- By…
- With…
- Through…
- Whether…
- From…
- The platform…
- The system…
- Users can…

Rewrite for syntactic variety.

## 7.3 Avoid excessive participial openings

High-risk:

- By combining…
- By leveraging…
- By integrating…
- By focusing…
- By adopting…
- By creating…

These often produce vague cause-and-effect claims.

## 7.4 Avoid stacked modifiers

Weak:

> A highly flexible, deeply intuitive, visually engaging, fully integrated platform.

Better:

> A platform with configurable workflows, a consistent interface, and integrations with the tools the team already uses.

## 7.5 Avoid adjective triplets

Agents frequently generate groups of three adjectives:

- clear, concise, and compelling
- simple, intuitive, and efficient
- bold, modern, and dynamic
- seamless, engaging, and user-friendly
- scalable, flexible, and robust

Triplets are not forbidden, but repeated triplets create a synthetic rhythm.

Use one precise adjective or replace the adjectives with evidence.

## 7.6 Do not create symmetry for its own sake

Weak:

> Users gain clarity, teams gain efficiency, and organizations gain impact.

Better:

> Users see what they need to do next. Teams spend less time clarifying incomplete requests.

## 7.7 Limit parenthetical overexplaining

Parentheses should add genuinely secondary information, not hold essential content.

Weak:

> The user can submit a request (which is then reviewed by the internal team before being approved).

Better:

> The internal team reviews each request before approval.

## 7.8 Use punctuation deliberately

Do not overuse:

- Em dashes
- Semicolons
- Colons in every paragraph
- Ellipses
- Exclamation marks
- Slash constructions
- Quotation marks around ordinary concepts

Prefer full stops and commas unless another mark improves meaning.

## 7.9 Avoid em-dash dependence

Agents often use em dashes to imitate editorial confidence.

Weak:

> The problem is not the number of tools—it is the lack of ownership.

Better:

> The problem is not the number of tools. It is the lack of ownership.

Or:

> The number of tools matters less than the lack of ownership.

## 7.10 Avoid sentence fragments used as fake emphasis

Weak:

> Faster approvals. Better collaboration. Stronger outcomes.

Better:

> The workflow reduces approval delays and keeps decisions attached to the request.

Fragments are acceptable in advertising, interface copy, scripts, and headings when the tone calls for them. Do not use them automatically.

# 8. Paragraph-Level Rules

## 8.1 Give each paragraph one job

A paragraph should do one of the following:

- State a finding
- Explain a mechanism
- Give an example
- Qualify a claim
- Compare options
- Describe a consequence
- Provide an instruction

Avoid paragraphs that perform all seven.

## 8.2 Lead with the useful point

Weak:

> In order to create a more effective and efficient workflow, it is essential to first understand how requests currently move through the organization.

Better:

> Map how requests currently move through the organization before designing the workflow.

## 8.3 Do not repeat the same point in multiple forms

AI-generated copy often states a point, paraphrases it, and then summarizes it.

Example of repetition:

> A centralized ticketing system creates a single source of truth. This means that all information is stored in one place. As a result, teams no longer need to search across multiple channels for updates.

Keep the strongest version:

> A centralized ticketing system keeps the brief, comments, decisions, files, and status history in one place.

## 8.4 Avoid mini-conclusions after every section

Do not end each section with:

- “This creates a more efficient process.”
- “This leads to better outcomes.”
- “This improves the overall user experience.”
- “Together, these elements…”
- “The result is…”
- “In this way…”

Let the section end on its most useful detail.

## 8.5 Do not force a paragraph into introduction, explanation, and conclusion

Short factual writing often needs only the explanation.

# 9. Structural Rules

## 9.1 Do not default to five-part essay structure

Agents frequently produce:

1. Broad introduction
2. Definition
3. Benefits
4. Challenges
5. Conclusion

Use the structure required by the task.

Examples:

For a recommendation:

1. Recommendation
2. Why
3. Tradeoffs
4. Implementation notes

For a critique:

1. Main problem
2. Evidence
3. Consequence
4. Proposed correction

For website copy:

1. User need
2. Offer
3. Proof
4. Action

For a process guide:

1. Preconditions
2. Steps
3. Exceptions
4. Completion criteria

## 9.2 Avoid unnecessary introductions

Do not begin by restating the request.

User asks:

> Explain why Astro is useful for content websites.

Weak opening:

> Astro is a modern web framework that has gained popularity for building fast and efficient content-focused websites.

Better:

> Astro makes sense when most pages are content rather than application state. It ships little or no client-side JavaScript by default, while still letting developers use React, Vue, Svelte, or other component frameworks where needed.

## 9.3 Avoid unnecessary conclusions

Do not summarize a short answer unless the summary adds a decision.

Weak ending:

> In conclusion, Astro is a powerful and flexible option for modern content websites.

Better ending:

> It is less suitable when the site behaves mainly like a logged-in web application with substantial client-side state.

## 9.4 Use headings only when they improve scanning

Do not turn every answer into a document.

Avoid headings such as:

- Introduction
- Understanding the Problem
- Key Considerations
- Benefits
- Final Thoughts
- Conclusion

Prefer descriptive headings:

- Why requests get stuck
- Required ticket fields
- Approval states
- What should stay outside the system

## 9.5 Avoid identical section shapes

If every section has:

- One-sentence intro
- Three bullets
- One-sentence conclusion

the document will feel generated.

Let structure follow content.

# 10. List Rules

## 10.1 Do not use a list when a sentence is clearer

Weak:

The form should include:

- Name
- Email
- Phone number

Better:

> The form should ask for name, email, and phone number.

Use lists when items need to be scanned, compared, completed, or referenced independently.

## 10.2 Avoid decorative lists

Do not convert ordinary prose into bullets merely to make it look organized.

## 10.3 Avoid perfectly parallel bullet lists unless the content requires it

Human-authored lists often vary slightly in length because the ideas vary.

Do not make every bullet:

- Start with a bold label
- Contain exactly two sentences
- End with a benefit statement

## 10.4 Avoid bold-label saturation

Weak:

- **Clarity:** Improve the clarity of the navigation.
- **Consistency:** Ensure consistency across pages.
- **Efficiency:** Increase efficiency for users.

Better:

- Group services by user task rather than internal department.
- Use the same labels in the menu, page titles, and breadcrumbs.
- Keep frequent actions visible without opening additional menus.

## 10.5 Do not force the number three

Agents overproduce groups of three. Use two, four, five, or seven when that is the real number.

---

# 11. Transition Rules

## 11.1 Remove transitions that do not carry meaning

High-risk transitions:

- Additionally
- Furthermore
- Moreover
- In addition
- However
- That said
- On the other hand
- Consequently
- Therefore
- Thus
- As such
- In contrast
- Meanwhile
- Ultimately

These are valid words. The problem is automatic use.

Often, a full stop is enough.

Weak:

> Additionally, the platform supports mobile devices.

Better:

> The platform also works on mobile devices.

Or simply:

> The platform works on mobile devices.

## 11.2 Do not overuse “However”

If the contrast is obvious, state it directly.

Weak:

> The tool is easy to set up. However, it may become difficult to maintain at scale.

Better:

> The tool is easy to set up but becomes harder to maintain as the number of workflows grows.

## 11.3 Avoid fake contrast

Weak:

> While the design is visually appealing, it is also highly functional.

Visual appeal and functionality are not natural opposites.

Better:

> The visual system is distinctive, but the navigation and forms still use familiar interaction patterns.

# 12. Tone Rules

## 12.1 Do not default to cheerful

The correct tone may be:

- Neutral
- Direct
- Formal
- Technical
- Restrained
- Reassuring
- Critical
- Instructional
- Conversational
- Promotional
- Ceremonial

Choose based on context.

## 12.2 Avoid simulated warmth

High-risk phrases:

- We are thrilled
- We are excited to
- We are delighted
- We are proud to
- It is our pleasure
- We believe passionately
- We care deeply
- Your journey matters
- You are not alone
- We are here for you every step of the way

These phrases are appropriate only when the organization can credibly support them.

Weak healthcare copy:

> We are here to support you every step of your journey.

Better:

> A care coordinator will contact you within two working days and explain the next steps.

## 12.3 Avoid false intimacy

Do not assume the user’s emotions.

Avoid:

- We know how frustrating this can be
- We understand your concerns
- You deserve better
- We know your time is valuable
- We get it
- We have all been there

Use only when the brand has a reason and the tone supports it.

## 12.4 Avoid fake authority

Do not write:

- The truth is
- The reality is
- Make no mistake
- There is no doubt
- Without question
- It is clear that

unless the claim is genuinely established.

## 12.5 Avoid motivational filler

- Take your business to the next level
- Unlock your full potential
- Turn your vision into reality
- Start your journey today
- The possibilities are endless
- Your future starts here
- Make an impact
- Dream bigger
- Achieve more

Replace with the actual action and outcome.

# 13. Marketing Copy Rules

## 13.1 Sell through specificity

Weak:

> Transform the way your team works.

Better:

> Replace scattered briefs, email approvals, and status meetings with one request queue and a visible decision history.

## 13.2 Avoid benefit stacking without proof

Weak:

> Save time, reduce costs, improve collaboration, and achieve better results.

Better:

> Reusable templates reduce setup time for recurring requests. Required fields also prevent account managers from submitting incomplete briefs.

## 13.3 Avoid universal claims

Do not say:

- for everyone
- for any business
- for teams of all sizes
- whatever your needs
- no matter your industry
- the perfect solution
- everything you need

Define the intended customer.

## 13.4 Use one clear promise

Do not make the hero section promise speed, trust, innovation, growth, simplicity, scale, flexibility, and transformation at once.

## 13.5 Avoid fake urgency

Do not use:

- Act now
- Do not miss out
- Limited time
- The clock is ticking
- Your competitors are already ahead

unless urgency is real and verifiable.

## 13.6 Do not confuse slogans with positioning

Weak:

> Building tomorrow, today.

Better:

> Compliance software for Greek accounting teams managing high-volume tax submissions.

## 13.7 Avoid generic calls to action

High-risk:

- Learn more
- Get started
- Discover more
- Explore
- Join us
- Take the next step
- Start your journey

Prefer the action:

- Compare plans
- Book a consultation
- Submit a request
- View available grants
- Download the guide
- Check eligibility
- See project details

# 14. UX and Product Copy Rules

## 14.1 Describe the user action, not the interface category

Weak:

> Continue to the next step.

Better:

> Review your details

Weak:

> Click here to proceed.

Better:

> Submit application

## 14.2 Avoid vague error messages

Weak:

> Something went wrong. Please try again.

Better:

> The file could not be uploaded because it exceeds the 10 MB limit.

## 14.3 Avoid blaming the user

Weak:

> You entered an invalid email.

Better:

> Enter an email in the format name@example.com.

## 14.4 Do not overexplain common actions

Weak:

> Click the button below to save your changes and return to the previous page.

Better button:

> Save changes

Supporting text only when needed:

> Your notification settings will apply to new tickets.

## 14.5 Avoid branded language inside functional flows

Do not force marketing tone into:

- Forms
- Error messages
- Password recovery
- Payment failure
- Consent
- Account deletion
- Support escalation

Clarity takes priority.

# 15. Corporate and Strategy Writing Rules

## 15.1 Replace “alignment” with the actual agreement

Weak:

> The workshop will create alignment around priorities.

Better:

> The workshop will end with an agreed priority order, named owners, and a decision on which requests will not enter the first release.

## 15.2 Replace “stakeholders” when the people can be named

Weak:

> Key stakeholders should be involved early.

Better:

> Include account management, design, development, support, and the person who approves scope changes.

## 15.3 Replace “best practices” with the practice

Weak:

> Follow accessibility best practices.

Better:

> Use semantic headings, keyboard-accessible controls, visible focus states, descriptive labels, and sufficient color contrast.

## 15.4 Avoid strategy fog

Weak:

> The strategy focuses on creating a future-ready ecosystem that drives sustainable growth.

Better:

> The strategy prioritizes recurring revenue, reduces reliance on one-off custom projects, and standardizes delivery around three service packages.

## 15.5 Distinguish goals, actions, outputs, and outcomes

Do not blur them.

- Goal: Reduce unresolved support requests.
- Action: Introduce triage rules and ownership.
- Output: A categorized queue with assigned owners.
- Outcome: Fewer requests remain unanswered after two working days.

## 15.6 Do not call a list a framework unless it behaves like one

A framework should define relationships, dimensions, stages, criteria, or decisions. A list of topics is not automatically a framework.

# 16. Academic and Analytical Writing Rules

## 16.1 Avoid inflated academic phrasing

Weak:

> It is imperative to acknowledge the multifaceted nature of the phenomenon.

Better:

> The phenomenon has legal, economic, and technical dimensions.

## 16.2 Do not use complexity as decoration

Avoid:

- multifaceted
- nuanced
- intricate
- complex interplay
- rich tapestry
- broader discourse
- critical lens
- evolving paradigm
- intersection of
- situated within

Use only when the complexity is then specified.

## 16.3 Do not claim novelty without evidence

Avoid:

- This study offers a novel perspective
- This research fills a critical gap
- This unprecedented analysis
- A unique contribution

State the contribution precisely.

> This study compares how three Greek public bodies translated the same EU accessibility requirement into procurement specifications.

## 16.4 Do not summarize sources with identical syntax

Mechanical:

> Smith argues that…
> Jones argues that…
> Brown argues that…

Better:

> Smith treats sovereignty as a matter of infrastructure ownership. Jones focuses instead on regulatory control, while Brown examines dependence on cloud providers.

## 16.5 Avoid “underscores,” “highlights,” and “demonstrates” repetition

Choose verbs based on what the evidence does:

- describes
- reports
- estimates
- compares
- identifies
- distinguishes
- associates
- documents
- proposes
- disputes
- supports
- contradicts
- suggests

## 16.6 Keep causal language disciplined

Correlation does not “prove,” “show that,” or “lead to” without support.

---

# 17. Editorial and Explanatory Writing Rules

## 17.1 Start near the point of interest

Weak:

> Since the beginning of human civilization, people have sought ways to communicate more effectively.

Better:

> Email became the default workplace record because it is searchable, asynchronous, and easy to forward. Those same strengths also make long approval chains difficult to manage.

## 17.2 Avoid generic scene-setting

Do not add history unless it explains the present issue.

## 17.3 Use examples that are plausible and specific

Weak:

> For example, a company could use AI to improve efficiency.

Better:

> A support team could use a classifier to route billing, technical, and cancellation requests into separate queues. A human should still review low-confidence cases.

## 17.4 Do not overuse rhetorical questions

Weak:

> But what does this mean for the future? And how can businesses prepare?

Better:

> For businesses, the immediate issue is data ownership. They need to know which customer information enters the model, where it is stored, and whether it is used for training.

## 17.5 Avoid the “hook, reveal, lesson” template unless writing social content

Not every paragraph needs suspense.

# 18. Social Media Copy Rules

## 18.1 Avoid fake confession hooks

High-risk:

- I have a confession
- I was wrong about…
- Nobody talks about…
- Here is the hard truth
- This changed everything
- I wish I knew this sooner
- Stop scrolling
- Read that again
- Let that sink in

Use only when factually and tonally justified.

## 18.2 Avoid engagement bait

- Agree?
- Thoughts?
- What do you think?
- Tag someone who needs this
- Save this for later
- Share if you agree
- Comment below

A question is acceptable when there is a real question.

## 18.3 Avoid fake vulnerability

Do not manufacture personal struggle for credibility.

## 18.4 Avoid line-break drama

Weak:

> The problem?
>
> It was never the tool.
>
> It was the process.

Better:

> The tool was not the main problem. The team had no clear process for ownership, approval, or scope changes.

## 18.5 Avoid overformatted emphasis

Do not capitalize ordinary words for effect:

> This is the ONE thing every designer needs to know.

# 19. Localization Rules

## 19.1 Write for the target language, not through English syntax

A translation should read as if originally written in the target language.

Do not preserve:

- English sentence order when unnatural
- English corporate idioms
- English punctuation habits
- English title capitalization
- English metaphor patterns
- Unnecessary loanwords
- Anglicized calls to action
- Word-for-word rhetorical structures

## 19.2 Do not translate AI-isms

English:

> Unlock the power of seamless collaboration.

A literal translation remains weak in any language.

Translate the meaning:

> Keep briefs, comments, files, and approvals in the same project record.

## 19.3 Respect local institutional vocabulary

For government, legal, educational, medical, and corporate writing, use the terms the target audience already encounters in official processes.

## 19.4 Avoid unnatural Greek corporate phrasing

In Greek copy, treat the following as high-risk unless context requires them:

- ξεκλειδώνουμε δυνατότητες
- αξιοποιούμε τη δύναμη
- αναβαθμίζουμε την εμπειρία
- δημιουργούμε αξία
- οδηγούμε την ανάπτυξη
- ολιστική προσέγγιση
- απρόσκοπτη εμπειρία
- δυναμικό οικοσύστημα
- ταξίδι του χρήστη
- στο επίκεντρο
- επόμενη ημέρα
- καινοτόμες λύσεις
- νέα εποχή
- με το βλέμμα στο μέλλον
- κάνουμε το όραμα πραγματικότητα
- ανταποκρίνεται στις σύγχρονες ανάγκες
- θέτουμε τον χρήστη στο κέντρο

Replace with the actual process, service, obligation, or user action.

Weak:

> Η νέα πλατφόρμα αναβαθμίζει την εμπειρία του πολίτη και τον τοποθετεί στο επίκεντρο.

Better:

> Ο πολίτης μπορεί να βρει την αρμόδια υπηρεσία, να υποβάλει το αίτημά του και να παρακολουθεί την πορεία του από την ίδια σελίδα.

# 20. Evidence and Specificity Rules

## 20.1 Every major adjective should answer “how?”

- Faster: by how much, or through what mechanism?
- Easier: which step was removed or clarified?
- Safer: what risk is reduced?
- Better: according to which criterion?
- More accessible: which barrier is addressed?
- More efficient: what work disappears?
- More consistent: which elements now follow the same rule?
- More flexible: what can change without redevelopment?
- More scalable: what volume, team size, or content growth is supported?

## 20.2 Use named constraints

Specific writing mentions:

- Audience
- Device
- Time
- Budget
- Location
- Role
- Workflow
- Volume
- Regulation
- Dependency
- Decision owner
- Success metric

Weak:

> The system should work well for different users.

Better:

> Account managers need a short intake form. Production leads need triage controls. Clients should only see tickets that require approval.

## 20.3 Prefer operational details

Weak:

> Improve communication.

Better:

> Keep approval comments attached to the version they refer to and record who approved it and when.

## 20.4 Do not invent specificity

Specificity must come from:

- The user’s brief
- Supplied documents
- Verified research
- Clearly marked assumptions
- Reasonable examples identified as examples

Never fabricate metrics, customer quotes, research findings, technical capabilities, deadlines, legal requirements, or implementation status.

# 22. Controlled Imperfection

Human writing is not improved by random mistakes.

Do not simulate humanity through:

- Typos
- Incorrect grammar
- Forced slang
- Random fragments
- Lowercase text without reason
- Excessive contractions
- Fake personal anecdotes
- Manufactured uncertainty
- Arbitrary sentence length
- Deliberately awkward phrasing

Instead, create naturalness through:

- Uneven but purposeful rhythm
- Context-specific word choice
- Selective detail
- Honest qualification
- Direct judgment
- Variation in structure
- Restraint
- Omission of unnecessary explanation

# 23. Rewrite Algorithm

Use the following process when editing AI-like copy.

## Pass 1: Extract the actual meaning

For each paragraph, identify:

- The main claim
- The evidence
- The consequence
- The action
- The intended reader

Delete everything that does not support one of these.

## Pass 2: Remove generic framing

Delete or rewrite:

- Broad introductions
- Empty context statements
- Self-evident observations
- Generic importance claims
- Meta-commentary about the content

## Pass 3: Replace abstractions

Underline words such as:

- solution
- experience
- value
- impact
- innovation
- strategy
- ecosystem
- journey
- efficiency
- engagement

Replace them with the actual object, action, or result.

## Pass 4: Check every adjective

For each adjective, ask:

- Is it measurable?
- Is it supported?
- Is it needed?
- Can it be replaced by a fact?

## Pass 5: Break symmetry

Check for:

- Repeated triplets
- Repeated sentence openings
- Identical bullet shapes
- Parallel paragraph lengths
- Formulaic section endings
- Repeated transition words

Vary only where variation improves the text.

## Pass 6: Tighten causality

Replace unsupported constructions such as:

- ensures
- guarantees
- leads to
- results in
- transforms
- drives

with accurate relationships:

- supports
- reduces
- makes it easier to
- is intended to
- is associated with
- can help
- removes
- adds
- records
- exposes

## Pass 7: Adjust tone

Remove:

- Automatic praise
- Fake warmth
- Exclamation marks
- Motivational language
- Unnecessary reassurance
- Overconfident claims

## Pass 8: Improve rhythm

Read the copy aloud.

Check whether:

- Every sentence is the same length
- Every paragraph sounds polished in the same way
- Commas are carrying too much
- Em dashes are being used as a default
- The prose sounds like a presentation voiceover

## Pass 9: End earlier

Delete the final sentence if it merely restates the paragraph.

Delete the conclusion if it merely summarizes the answer.

Delete the service offer unless required.

## Pass 10: Run the substitution test

Replace the main nouns with nouns from another industry.

If the text still works unchanged, it is probably too generic.

Example:

> Our innovative solution empowers teams to unlock efficiency and drive meaningful outcomes.

This works for software, consulting, healthcare, education, logistics, finance, and almost anything else. It fails the test.

# 24. AI-Ism Detection Checklist

Before delivering copy, inspect it for the following.

## Language

- [ ] No broad “In today’s…” opening
- [ ] No unnecessary “more than just” construction
- [ ] No repeated “not only, but also”
- [ ] No generic “whether you are” expansion
- [ ] No automatic “at its core”
- [ ] No filler “ultimately”
- [ ] No “it is important to note” unless genuinely needed
- [ ] No repeated “this ensures,” “this allows,” or “this enables”
- [ ] No corporate verbs without concrete meaning
- [ ] No unsupported superlatives
- [ ] No repeated adjective triplets
- [ ] No fake urgency
- [ ] No motivational filler
- [ ] No automatic praise
- [ ] No default service offer at the end

## Structure

- [ ] The first sentence contains useful information
- [ ] The answer does not restate the request
- [ ] Headings describe content rather than document structure
- [ ] Lists are used only where scanning helps
- [ ] The number of bullets reflects the real content
- [ ] Sections do not all have the same shape
- [ ] No repeated mini-conclusions
- [ ] The final paragraph adds information
- [ ] The conclusion is necessary

## Specificity

- [ ] Abstract nouns are defined
- [ ] Major adjectives are supported
- [ ] Claims identify the relevant user, process, or constraint
- [ ] Benefits explain the mechanism
- [ ] Examples are plausible and context-specific
- [ ] No invented facts or metrics
- [ ] Assumptions are marked
- [ ] The copy could not be reused unchanged for an unrelated client

## Tone

- [ ] Tone matches the audience and channel
- [ ] Confidence matches the evidence
- [ ] Warmth is earned
- [ ] The copy does not pretend to know the reader’s feelings
- [ ] No fake authority
- [ ] No emotional exaggeration
- [ ] No accidental marketing tone in functional copy

## Rhythm

- [ ] Sentence openings vary
- [ ] Sentence lengths vary naturally
- [ ] Em dashes are limited
- [ ] Transitions are not mechanical
- [ ] Paragraphs do not repeat the same cadence
- [ ] Parallelism is used deliberately
- [ ] The text sounds natural when read aloud

# 25. Scoring Rubric

Score each category from 0 to 4.

## 25.1 Specificity

**0**: Almost entirely generic  
**1**: Some context, mostly abstract  
**2**: Several concrete details, but key claims remain vague  
**3**: Most claims identify real users, actions, constraints, or evidence  
**4**: Every major point is grounded and operational

## 25.2 Naturalness

**0**: Strongly formulaic  
**1**: Frequent AI-like phrases and symmetry  
**2**: Readable but noticeably generated  
**3**: Mostly natural with minor generic phrasing  
**4**: Credible human rhythm and judgment

## 25.3 Economy

**0**: Repetitive and inflated  
**1**: Multiple removable sections  
**2**: Some repetition and filler  
**3**: Tight with minor excess  
**4**: Every sentence has a clear function

## 25.4 Tone Fit

**0**: Wrong tone for audience or channel  
**1**: Generic professional tone  
**2**: Partly adapted  
**3**: Strong fit with minor inconsistencies  
**4**: Fully aligned with audience, purpose, and context

## 25.5 Evidence Discipline

**0**: Unsupported or fabricated claims  
**1**: Frequent overstatement  
**2**: Mixed certainty  
**3**: Mostly calibrated  
**4**: Claims, assumptions, and evidence are clearly distinguished

## 25.6 Structural Fit

**0**: Template structure unrelated to task  
**1**: Generic introduction-body-conclusion  
**2**: Useful structure with formulaic sections  
**3**: Structure mostly follows the information  
**4**: Structure is fully adapted to the task

### Minimum acceptance score

A final answer should normally score at least:

- 3 in every category
- 20 out of 24 overall

For public-facing brand copy, aim for 22 or higher.

For legal, medical, financial, academic, or policy content, Evidence Discipline must score 4.

# 26. Before-and-After Examples

## Example 1: Product description

AI-like:

> Our cutting-edge platform empowers teams to streamline workflows, enhance collaboration, and drive meaningful results through a seamless and intuitive experience.

Rewritten:

> The platform keeps requests, comments, files, approvals, and status changes in one record. Teams can see who owns the next action and which decisions are still pending.

Why the rewrite is stronger:

- Names the objects
- Names the workflow
- Avoids inflated claims
- Explains the mechanism
- Removes generic adjectives

## Example 2: Website redesign rationale

AI-like:

> The new design creates a modern, engaging, and user-friendly experience that reflects the organization’s values while making information easier to access.

Rewritten:

> The redesign moves the three most-used services to the first screen, replaces internal department labels with user-facing categories, and applies the same navigation pattern across desktop and mobile.

## Example 3: Strategy statement

AI-like:

> Our strategy is centered around innovation, collaboration, and sustainable growth.

Rewritten:

> The company will standardize three repeatable services, reduce custom one-off work, and assign one owner to each client account.

## Example 4: Government service copy

AI-like:

> The new digital service puts citizens at the heart of a seamless and accessible experience.

Rewritten:

> Citizens can check eligibility, upload the required documents, and follow the application status without visiting an office.

## Example 5: Accessibility statement

AI-like:

> We are committed to providing an inclusive and accessible digital experience for everyone.

Rewritten:

> The website supports keyboard navigation, visible focus states, text resizing, descriptive form labels, and sufficient color contrast. Accessibility issues can be reported through the contact form.

## Example 6: Design critique

AI-like:

> While the page has a strong visual foundation, there are opportunities to improve hierarchy, consistency, and usability.

Rewritten:

> The page gives the hero image and the service cards equal visual weight, so the primary action is difficult to identify. Reduce the card contrast, shorten the introductory copy, and move the application button above the fold.

## Example 7: Technical recommendation

AI-like:

> Astro is a powerful and flexible framework that enables developers to build fast, scalable, and modern websites.

Rewritten:

> Astro is a good fit for content-heavy sites because it renders pages with little client-side JavaScript by default. It becomes less attractive when most screens depend on persistent client-side state or application-style interactions.

## Example 8: Internal email

AI-like:

> I hope this message finds you well. I wanted to reach out regarding the current status of the project and ensure that we are aligned on the next steps moving forward.

Rewritten:

> Please confirm whether the homepage copy is approved. Development cannot continue with the remaining templates until the final section order and calls to action are agreed.

## Example 9: Proposal language

AI-like:

> We will work closely with your team to craft a tailored solution that meets your unique needs and delivers long-term value.

Rewritten:

> The first phase includes stakeholder interviews, a content audit, and two prototype rounds. The final scope will reflect the workflows and approval constraints identified during that phase.

## Example 10: Social post

AI-like:

> Big things are coming. We are excited to share a game-changing new chapter in our journey. Stay tuned.

Rewritten:

> We are opening a second support center in Patras in September. Recruitment for customer support and technical operations begins next week.

# 27. Patterns That Are Acceptable in Moderation

Do not turn this guide into a crude blacklist.

The following may be correct when used deliberately:

- “However” for real contrast
- “Ultimately” for a final decision after analysis
- “Seamless” for genuinely uninterrupted cross-system behavior
- “Robust” for fault tolerance, validation, or technical resilience
- “Scalable” when scale dimensions are defined
- “Journey” in formal service-design contexts
- “Ecosystem” when describing an actual network of dependent actors and systems
- “Holistic” when contrasting a whole-system approach with isolated interventions
- Three-part lists when there are exactly three relevant items
- Parallelism for emphasis
- Fragments in campaigns or interface copy
- Rhetorical questions in speeches or editorial content
- Warmth in support, healthcare, hospitality, and community communication
- Strong claims when supported by evidence
- A conclusion in long analytical writing
- A follow-up offer in an explicitly collaborative workflow

The rule is not “never use common language.”

The rule is “do not use it automatically.”

# 29. Prompt-Level Instruction for Other Agents

The following block can be added to a system prompt or project instruction file.

```md
## Counter-AI-Ism Writing Rules

Write for the actual reader, task, and context. Do not produce generic “good writing.”

Avoid broad introductions, automatic praise, corporate abstraction, empty benefit language, adjective triplets, repeated three-part lists, fake contrast, motivational filler, unsupported claims, formulaic conclusions, and default offers to continue helping.

Do not use phrases such as “in today’s fast-paced world,” “more than just,” “at its core,” “unlock the power,” “seamless and intuitive,” “navigate the landscape,” “pave the way,” or “ultimately, the choice depends on your needs” unless they are specifically justified.

Prefer concrete nouns and verbs. Name the user, action, object, constraint, decision, or outcome. Replace claims such as “improves efficiency” with the exact work that becomes faster, disappears, or moves to a different role.

Do not describe copy as clear, compelling, engaging, strategic, robust, innovative, accessible, scalable, or user-friendly without explaining why.

Vary sentence length and structure naturally. Do not make every paragraph symmetrical or every list contain three items. Limit em dashes, rhetorical questions, fragments, semicolons, and decorative transitions.

Start near the point. End when the answer is complete. Do not restate the user’s request, add an unnecessary conclusion, or append “let me know if you need anything else.”

Match certainty to evidence. Do not invent facts, quotes, metrics, user behavior, legal requirements, technical capabilities, or research findings. Mark assumptions.

For rewrites, preserve the meaning while removing generic framing, inflated language, repeated ideas, unnatural smoothness, and English-derived phrasing that does not fit the target language.

Before finalizing, ask:
1. Could this sentence appear in hundreds of unrelated AI outputs?
2. Does every major adjective have evidence?
3. Does every paragraph add a new point?
4. Is the structure adapted to the task?
5. Could the ending be removed?
6. Does the copy sound natural when read aloud?
```

# 32. Compact Review Commands

These commands can be used inside an agent workflow.



# 33. Failure Modes to Avoid

## 33.1 Overcorrection into bluntness

Removing AI-isms does not mean removing courtesy, nuance, or context.

Bad:

> Send the file.

Better:

> Please send the final logo file in SVG format so it can be used in the responsive header.

## 33.2 Overcorrection into minimalism

Not every sentence must be short.

Complex ideas may require subordinate clauses, definitions, qualifications, and examples.

## 33.3 Overcorrection into slang

Professional naturalness is not the same as casual speech.

## 33.4 Overcorrection into negativity

A critical tone should identify problems without sounding contemptuous.

## 33.5 Overcorrection into arbitrary variation

Do not replace clear repetition with confusing synonyms.

For interface and technical writing, consistency is often more important than lexical variety.

## 33.6 Overcorrection into unsupported certainty

Direct writing still needs calibrated claims.

## 33.7 Overcorrection into anti-structure

Useful headings, lists, and summaries should remain when they help the reader.

# 34. Domain-Specific Acceptance Tests

## Website copy

- Does the page say what the organization does within the first screen?
- Is the audience identifiable?
- Are the claims supported?
- Is the call to action specific?
- Could a competitor use the same copy unchanged?
- Are important terms the words users search for?
- Does the page avoid generic innovation language?

## Product documentation

- Does each instruction begin with the actual action?
- Are prerequisites stated?
- Are expected results described?
- Are errors and exceptions covered?
- Are interface labels reproduced accurately?
- Is terminology consistent?

## Internal communication

- Is the request clear?
- Is the owner named?
- Is the deadline explicit?
- Is the blocking decision identified?
- Can the recipient act without asking what is meant?

## Strategy writing

- Are goals separated from actions?
- Are priorities ranked?
- Are tradeoffs acknowledged?
- Are excluded options named?
- Are owners and decision criteria visible?
- Does “strategy” contain actual choices?

## Public-sector copy

- Can the citizen identify eligibility?
- Are required documents named?
- Are deadlines and legal conditions clear?
- Is the next action explicit?
- Are internal departmental terms avoided where possible?
- Is the tone neutral and non-promotional?

## Academic writing

- Are claims sourced?
- Is causality disciplined?
- Is novelty described rather than announced?
- Are concepts defined?
- Are sources compared rather than listed?
- Is jargon necessary?

## Social copy

- Is the opening specific?
- Is the claim credible?
- Is the formatting natural?
- Is there a real reason to comment or click?
- Does the post avoid manufactured vulnerability and engagement bait?

# 35. Final Standard

Good counter-AI-ist copy should feel as though someone:

- Understood the actual situation
- Chose what mattered
- Left out what did not
- Knew the audience
- Had a reason for the structure
- Was willing to make a judgment
- Did not overstate
- Did not imitate enthusiasm
- Did not decorate weak ideas
- Stopped when the work was complete

The objective is not to hide that an AI agent was involved.

The objective is to ensure the copy earns the reader’s attention through relevance, precision, and judgment rather than through familiar patterns of generated prose.
